# Research Note 08 – Ranking, Targeting, and the Validity of Proxy Measures

## Concept Connection
Sorting and ranking operations are the computational foundation of 
resource targeting. In a financial fragility study, ranking households 
by a fragility proxy is how policy recommendations translate into 
concrete action: which households receive intervention when resources 
are finite. The Survey of Consumer Finances analysis will rely on 
ranking to identify the most fragile households and to validate 
whether the constructed fragility measure aligns with intuitive and 
theoretical expectations.

## Paper-Relevant Insight
Today's lesson surfaced a critical methodological principle: the 
technical act of ranking is trivial, but the choice of ranking 
variable is a substantive research decision with real consequences. 
Ranking households by debt-to-income identifies a specific population 
that may differ substantially from the population identified by 
ranking on liquid-assets-to-income, savings rate, or a composite 
index.

This has direct implications for the capstone:
- The fragility measure used for ranking must be theoretically 
  justified, not chosen for convenience
- Different proxy measures will produce different "most fragile" 
  populations; these differences must be examined explicitly
- A household ranked highly fragile on one measure may rank low on 
  another; the paper must address this sensitivity

## Methodological Direction (for paper)
The capstone will:
1. Construct multiple candidate fragility measures (debt-to-income, 
   liquid-assets-to-income, savings rate, composite index)
2. Rank households under each measure
3. Examine the overlap and divergence between the top-fragile 
   populations under each measure
4. Report how sensitive the "most fragile" classification is to the 
   choice of proxy
5. Justify the final fragility measure with explicit theoretical and 
   empirical reasoning

## Methodological Note
Targeting policy on a single proxy risks systematically misdirecting 
resources if the proxy is a poor measure of the underlying construct. 
A household with a high debt-to-income ratio but substantial liquid 
savings may be less fragile than a household with a moderate ratio 
and no emergency buffer. The validity of the ranking depends entirely 
on the validity of the ranking variable as a measure of true 
fragility. This is a measurement-validity problem, and it will be 
treated explicitly in the paper.
